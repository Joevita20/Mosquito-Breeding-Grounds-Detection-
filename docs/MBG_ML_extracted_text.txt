I. INTRODUCTION
Mosquito-borne diseases pose a serious threat to globalpublic health, with millions of cases reported annually.These diseases, such as malaria, dengue, and yellowfever,are transmitted to humans through the bites of infectedfemale mosquitoes. One crucial strategy for controlling thespread of these diseases is to identify and eliminatemosquito breeding grounds. However, traditional methodsof identifying breeding sites, such as visual inspection, areoften time-consuming and labor-intensive. In recent years,deep learning models demonstrated great potential fordetectingmosquitobreedinggroundsusingUAVimagery.
Mosquitoes can start breeding when stagnant orslow-moving water is present. Thiswater canaccumulateinvarious places, such as puddles, buckets, flower pots, oldtires, gutters, bird baths, water tanksandothers. Mosquitoescan also breed in natural bodies of water such as ponds,swamps, and marshes. It is relatively commontofinditemsthat can gather stagnant water and act asmosquitobreedinggrounds. As a result, managing mosquito populationswithout the right technological assistance can be difficultandtime-consuming. Combiningtheexpertiseof aspecialistwith devices that can swiftly detect possible mosquitobreeding locations can be quite beneficial in suchcircumstances. Such tools can speed up the hunt formosquito foci, leading to more effective and efficientcontrol strategies.Health officials are required to inspect properties inorder to identify and remove potential mosquito breedinggrounds. However, therearedrawbackstothisstrategy, suchas financial, expense, and frequency and time restraints.Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) offer a number ofadvantages over conventional methods, making them afeasible alternative to satellite photography, which is notpractical due to their low spatial and temporal resolutionsand high cost. Videos and images can be captured usingUAVs at higher spatial and temporal resolutions, from a
variety of angles, andat higher altitudes. Theyminimizetheprobabilities that officials will come across dangerouscircumstances or locations. Although purchasing UAVequipment has a somewhat significant initial investment, itpays off because broad areas may be covered by a smallteamat lowoperational expenses.Deep learning models have shown great promise inaddressing this issue. In this paper, we propose a novelapproach to mosquito breed ground detection using deeplearning models, including YOLO, Mask R-CNN, andTransformers. We comparetheperformanceof thesemodelson a Mosquito Breed Ground (MBG) dataset using variousoptimizers and evaluate their Mean Average Precision(MAP) as a measure of accuracy. Our results showthat thedeep learning models outperform traditional methods formosquito breed ground detection and achieve a high MAPof 0.92.Themajor contributionsof thispaper includes:● Used GANtogenerateanewset of trainingimagesbecause of the insufficient availability of fewclasses.● Trained models with different backbones to detectthemosquitobreedgrounds.● Applied various optimizers on the models toincreasetheaccuracyof detection.The rest of this paper is organizedasfollows: SectionIIprovides an overview of related work in the area ofmosquito breed ground detection. Thebackbone and the detection layers are thetwoprimarypartsof the YOLOv8 architecture.The Darknet architecture, adeep neural network made up of convolutional layers thatextract features from the input image, serves as thebackbone of the system. The processing of the incomingimage and creation of a set of feature maps utilized by thedetection layers to forecast item bounding boxes and classprobabilities is the responsibility of the backbone. Agroupof convolutional layers that anticipate the bounding boxesfor each object in the input image make up the detectionlayers. In order to accomplish this, YOLOv8 makes use ofanchor boxes, which are pre-defined boxes of various sizesand aspect ratios that serve as a starting point fordetermining the location of objects in an image. A set ofpotential boundingboxesfor eachitemclassiscreatedusingfeature maps and anchor boxes.The relevant feature mapsare then used to determine the class probabilities for eachcandidate bounding box. By choosing the candidate boxeswith the highest probabilities and doing non-maximumsuppression to eliminate overlapping boxes, the finalprojected bounding boxes and class probabilities areachieved.c) Detectron2Detectron2isahighlyversatileandcustomizableobjectdetection and segmentation framework that providesawiderange of pre-trainedmodels, backbones, anddetectionheadsfor researchers and developers to choose from. Theframework is based on PyTorch and features a modulararchitecture that allows for flexible combinations ofbackbones and detection heads. Thebackboneof amodel isresponsible for featureextractionandcanbeselectedfromarange of pre-trained models such as ResNet, ResNeXt, andFPN. The feature maps generated by the backbone arethenpassed through a neck, which consists of a series ofconvolutional layers that perform feature fusion betweendifferent scales of the feature maps. The output of the neckis then fed into the detection head, which is responsiblefor

Modern object detection features andtechniques including RetinaNet, Faster R-CNN, and MaskR-CNN are all included in Detectron2. These pre-definedarchitectures can be used as detection heads and can becombinedwithdifferent backbonestocreatecustommodels.Additionally, the framework provides a range of tools andutilitiesfor datapreparation, model training, andevaluation.d) DETRDETR(DEtectionTRansformer) isarecent paradigminthe field of object detection that has attracted a lot ofattention due to its transformer-based architecture, which iscomparable to thoseusedinNLP. Aseriesof object queries,along with their accompanying class probabilities andbounding boxes, are produced by DETRafter receiving animage as input. DETRdoes not require the usageof anchorboxes, which can take time to set up when compared toother object identificationmodels.Anencoder andadecoderare the two primary parts of the DETR architecture. Theencoder is used to extract featuresfromtheinput imageandis based on a pre-trained ResNet architecture. Then, aftergoingthroughanumber of transformer layers, thesefeaturesare sent to the object queries, enabling better interactionbetween the features and the object queries. The objectqueries, along with the associated class probabilities andbounding boxes, are produced by the decoder's multiplemulti-head attention and feed-forward layers. Each objectquery must be assigned to a single object in the image, andthe decoder must anticipate the appropriate class andbounding box. Contrary to conventional object detectionmodels, DETR's transformer-based architecture offers anumber of benefits. It does not require the usage of anchorboxes, which can be challenging and time-consumingtosetup, and it allows for more flexible interactions between thefeaturesandtheobject queries.C.
